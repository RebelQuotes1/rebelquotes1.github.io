Top 10 Items to Catch Wild Game

When the grocery shelves are empty and the only meat left is the kind that still has a heartbeat, these ten items might mean the difference between starving... and feasting like a warlord.

1. Snare Wire

Simple. Silent. Savage. A few coils of snare wire can bag squirrels, rabbits, or that unlucky raccoon snooping around your shelter. Twist it. Anchor it. Wait.

2. Steel Traps

From mink to muskrat, steel traps don’t play around. Set them right and check them often. Humane? Maybe not. Effective? Hell yes.

3. Fishing Line & Hooks

Sometimes catching meat means thinking like a fish. Lightweight and portable, fishing gear should live in every bug-out bag. Bonus: can double as dental floss. Don’t ask.

4. Slingbow or Slingshot

Silent killers. Take down birds or rabbits without waking the whole damn forest. Accurate hands only—no YouTube warrior skills here.

5. Crossbow

Expensive, heavy, and completely worth it when you drop a deer from 50 yards in dead silence. Just don’t forget extra bolts, dummy.

6. Deadfall Components

Rock. Stick. Bait. Death. The deadfall trap is ancient and brutal. A well-placed chunk of stone can crush your dinner—and your fingers if you’re careless.

7. Bait Attractants

Marshmallows, peanut butter, or even a dirty sock soaked in bacon grease. If it stinks or tastes sweet, something will come sniffing. Use it.

8. Game Call (or Your Voice)

Turkey gobble, rabbit distress, fawn bleat. Learn the sounds, mimic the cries, and be the predator. Or carry a cheap game call if you’re tone-deaf.

9. Headlamp with Red Light

You’ll be checking traps before dawn and gutting game after dusk. Red light keeps your night vision sharp—and keeps you stealthy.

10. Butcher Knife & Gloves

Because what’s the point of catching dinner if you can’t process it? Sharp steel and nitrile gloves keep you clean, fast, and precise.

Don’t rely on one trick. Layer your approach. Set traps, stalk quietly, and be patient. In the wild, the loud and lazy don’t eat.
